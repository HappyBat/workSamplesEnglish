Security of the Baltic Sea Region

The name of the Baltic Sea region does not only refer to geography – for centuries it has been facilitating flows of ideas, goods and people. Serving as an important uniting factor, it has also been an endless source of protracted conflicts and wars. In the ambiance of a strategic contest between the West and Russia, the security of the Baltic Sea region seems to be very fragile today – even more at risk than ever before. To decrease the tension we should define the biggest challenges that the main actors in the region face today. So, what are they for Germany, Poland and Russia?

Long Haul Through Decennaries

Today, Russian-Western relations are in the most dramatic crisis and the impact of these problems is especially obvious in the Baltic Sea region (BSR). From the security point of view, the BSR is very important and recently seemed to be one of the most promising areas for cooperation of the West with Russia. Unfortunately, we see what we see – in 2019 it is one of the most risky and dangerous regions in Europe. At the same time, the history of this complex and sensitive region does not let forget the past contradictions and only exacerbates the current confrontation.

After 2014 it became obvious that all the attempts to develop a common security structure over the past 20 years have largely failed. It refers not only to unsuccessful tries to bind Russia but also to multiple tryings to provide regional security focusing on security-related institutions. The recent case of Crimea has led to the reassessment of NATO collective defense arrangements. While since 1991 the status quo was maintained, later it became clear that certain political actions can rapidly lead to military conflict. A difficult partnership became an outright confrontation.

Inevitable Conflict

BSR, being the “eastern flank” of NATO today, is an interdependent coalescence of eight Nordic and Baltic states. Despite their steady growth and successful cooperation, they are quite vulnerable, as the region is a key area both for Russia and the West: it is a buffer zone and a probable war theatre, where on Gotland or the Aland Islands an offensive operation could possibly start.

Nevertheless, countries take completely different stances in their viewpoints on the problem of BSR. In Russia’s opinion, the spread of Western institutions along its borders is an existential hazard. Meanwhile, Western counties see it as the expansion of the rule-based international order. This contradiction is obvious not only in BSR – the dissimilar interpretation of globalization and democracy provokes a clash of interests even far from the Baltic Sea. But today their misunderstanding can lead not merely to a moderate response as it was the foundation of post-Cold War order but to devastating consequences of military conflict.

Time for Recriminations

The tension in the region has already increased and the risks to stability are visible. They will unlikely take the form of an open military conflict but risks will definitely have direct and indirect impacts on its future. Both sides blame each other for this situation – Russia perceives itself as a target of Western aggression while the West accuses Russia of military interventions. Also, Russia, Germany and Poland have different opinions about NATO and EU enlargement in the region. Russia sees the region as a field for competition for power and NATO sees “deterring Russia” as a reason for development in the region. The region today is in grey zone between war and peace – thanks to military exercises and hybrid hazards, that became asymmetrical.

Besides, the impact of the US on the trilateral relations between Germany, Poland and Russia, like the recent opening of the military base ‘Fort Trump” in Poland, can only boost Russian mistrust to allies, when they engage on talks about mutual confidence. In fact, the attempts of the allies to promote regional stability are also torpedoed by the lack of political consensus on NATO membership – when Poland accuses Germany of spending much less GDP for defense than required, it does not rise the interest of other countries in deeper integration. What is more, Finland and Sweden are still not covered by NATO defense clause – it is one more destabilizing factor that makes the situation more erratic.

Light at End of Tunnel

However, the prospects are not as negative as it may look: a number of challenges should be overcome but there are a number of issues that can be implemented in the near future. Definitely, the process of implementation can be started with deepening of cooperation in topics of common interest: investments into long-term forms of cooperation, from ecology to civil interaction, seem to be a good idea. Long-term forms can also include developing better joint situational awareness that is crucial in the ambiance of perpetual military exercises. Expanding the exchange of information can help us in conflict management.

In a short-term prospective, both sides should try to enhance the role of OSCE in the region as a mighty mediator. In general, the stress must be on the cooperation not only inside, but also outside the region – the main idea for today is to renew US security guarantees within NATO, that are under risk because of Trump’s politics. The last but not the least, is to increase the integration of Finland and Sweden that are key regional actors. Probably, it will help to achieve a triangular balance in the region – security should be cooperative as well as common.
