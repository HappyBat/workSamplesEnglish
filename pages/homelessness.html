Homelessness In Cardiff Still On The Rise. Why?


The number of rough sleepers in Wales has risen by more than 30 percent between 2015 and 2019, counties scoring the highest being Cardiff and Wrexham. This is according to the numbers on homelessness provided by the Welsh Government on their website. However, the government states that the data, which is gathered by Local Authorities, is not comparable over time “due to timing differences.”

The Housing Options Service, a service contacted by people who have no home or are under threat of becoming homeless, deals with homelessness on a daily basis and the numbers they observe speak the same language. John*, project manager at the facility, stated that back in 2015 there were about 40 people living on the street. Back then, that was already a peak. In 2019, however, they counted about a hundred people sleeping rough. These numbers correspond well to the official statistics, leaving us with the question why all efforts in detaining homelessness remain powerless against ‘The Big Issue’.

John sees the main reasons for this in cuts to benefits and the lack of mental health intervention and support services, which were much better in the past. Asked about the typical ‘service user’ coming to the house he says it was white males, dealing with substance abuse, meaning alcohol and drugs. Their situation is often a result of financial problems and traumatic childhoods. Moreover, people using the emergency hotline run by the service, are often experiencing domestic violence. There are five times more men than women using the service and the vast majority is dealing with mental health problems such as depression or anxieties, both as a cause but also as a result of their homelessness.

Contrary to what some might believe though, the government’s efforts were much increased in recent years. At the Housing Centre, for instance, it funded a multi-disciplinary team consisting of counsellors, outreach workers – who are the key workers on the street – and others doing advocacy and mentoring. Various charities come together at the Service now. Overall, tackling homelessness in Cardiff has been more centralised in organisation, making it more efficient.

All this sounds very good but where is the catch then? The measures are addressing the symptoms instead of addressing the causes. According to part 2 of the Housing Wales Act of 2014 (with its changes of 2015), Local Authorities have an obligation to provide housing for people under threat of becoming homeless - and that is exactly what they do. The 2019 spot checks tell us that about 65% of cases were successfully prevented from homelessness. It seems, they should rather foster prevention even before that. John laments that there are not enough pre-emptive measures to intervene drug use and help with mental health: “We need drastic changes in how the system works” or, in other words, just to “put more money in where it should be.”


*Fictional name, since interviewee requested not to be named.
